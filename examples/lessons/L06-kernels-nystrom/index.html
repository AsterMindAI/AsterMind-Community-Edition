When a straight line just can't

L02 trained a classifier on text greetings — the model figured it out, no drama. That's because language has lots of features that line up neatly with class. Underneath, the model drew a (high-dimensional) straight line through feature space, with English on one side and French on the other.

Now picture a 2D dataset like this:

     class 0 (inner ring)         class 1 (outer ring)
         . . .                          . . .
       .       .                      .       .
      .  . . .  .                    .  ⊙ ⊙ ⊙  .
       .       .                      .       .
         . . .                          . . .
                                ↑ same center, larger radius

No straight line in (x, y) space separates an inner ring from an outer ring. Try drawing one — wherever you put it, half of each ring is on the wrong side.

This is the canonical "linearly inseparable" geometry. Real-world data has versions of this all over the place: pixel intensities for digits, audio spectra for words, cybersecurity event sequences. Linear classifiers fail; we need something stronger.

The kernel trick

What if we mapped each 2D point into a higher-dimensional space where the rings are linearly separable?

For the rings, an obvious lift is:

(x, y)  →  (x, y, x² + y²)   // add radius² as a third dim

In 3D, the inner ring sits at low x² + y²; the outer ring at high. A flat plane separates them. Solved.

The problem: we don't always know what mapping to use, and useful mappings are often very high-dimensional (sometimes infinite). Computing them explicitly is expensive or impossible.

The kernel trick: we never compute the mapping. We compute only pairwise inner products in the lifted space — and we get those for free from a kernel function.

K(a, b)  =  ⟨φ(a), φ(b)⟩   // for some implicit φ we never name

Algorithms that depend only on inner products (linear regression with a tweak — that's what KernelELM does) get the benefit of the lifted space without the cost. That's the whole idea.

RBF — local similarity

The most-used kernel is RBF (radial basis function):

K(a, b) = exp(-γ · ||a - b||²)

Read it as: "how close are a and b?"

  • a = b → distance 0 → K = 1.0 (max similarity)
  • a, b far apart → distance large → K → 0 (no similarity)

The hyperparameter γ controls the bandwidth: large γ = sharply local (only very close points feel similar); small γ = wider influence.

Mental model: imagine a Gaussian bump centered on each training point. The model decides a new point's class by which training points it's "close to" in the RBF sense. Curved decision regions emerge naturally — they're the boundaries between sets of overlapping bumps.

What kernels cost

Kernels are powerful, but pairwise inner products mean computing the kernel between every pair of training points. With N training points:

  • Build the N × N kernel matrix → O(N²) kernel evaluations.
  • Solve the regularized linear system on it → O(N³) arithmetic.
  • Predict: kernel evaluations against all N training points per test point.

Numbers to ground this:

  • N = 100 → 10,000 kernel evals. Trivial.
  • N = 10,000 → 100,000,000 evals + a 10,000³ solve. Doable with care.
  • N = 1,000,000 → 10¹² evals. Off the table.

Approximations let us keep the kernel benefit at lower cost. Today: Nyström. Tomorrow (in your capstone notebook): Random Fourier Features.

Nyström — ask a few representatives

Instead of computing the full N × N kernel matrix, pick a small number of landmark points (call it m, where m << N). Then:

  1. Compute kernel values from each training point to each landmark only — that's an N × m matrix.
  2. Use linear algebra (SVD-based whitening, in our implementation) to reconstruct the rest.
  3. Train as if we had the full kernel, but at O(N · m) cost instead of O(N²).

The intuition: landmarks act as "representative neighbors." Most training points are well-described by their kernel similarity to a few landmarks; you don't need to compute every pairwise comparison.

The tradeoff is approximation quality:

  • Small m (5, 10) → very fast; loses accuracy if landmarks happen to miss important structure.
  • Large m (~100) → close to exact-RBF accuracy; still much faster than full kernel.
  • m → N → identical to exact RBF (and identically expensive).

In You-Try 3 you'll find the right m for this dataset. For different problems the answer is different — there's no universal "best m."

// Nyström: pick m landmarks, compute kernel against landmarks
// RFF:     sample D random projections (cos/sin), use as features

// Same goal: get RBF-like accuracy at linear cost
